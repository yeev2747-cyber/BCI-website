Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) aim to connect the human brain with technology by placing computational devices near neurons. This concept represents the long-term vision of BCI research.

To understand the history of BCIs, we can begin with early discoveries about electrical activity in living organisms. In the 1790s, Luigi Galvani discovered the concept of animal electricity through experiments showing that electrical stimulation could cause frog muscles to move.

Many years later, in the 1920s, German psychiatrist Hans Berger developed electroencephalography (EEG). This technology allowed scientists to measure electrical activity from the human brain using electrodes placed outside the skull, creating one of the earliest methods for recording brain signals.

In the 1940s, researchers began using microelectrodes inserted into the cortex to record activity from individual neurons. These approaches showed that recording closer to the source could provide higher-resolution and higher-fidelity neural signals.

During the 1950s, scientists Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley developed mathematical models explaining how neurons communicate through electrical and chemical processes. Their work on the ionic mechanisms of nerve cells contributed greatly to neuroscience and later earned them the Nobel Prize.

In 1969, Edward Fetz from the University of Washington published important research demonstrating one of the earliest examples of a closed-loop Brain-Computer Interface. He recorded activity from individual neurons in monkeys and showed that neural activity could be influenced through feedback and reward systems.

Following these discoveries, BCI research expanded rapidly. Scientists developed new tools for studying the brain, improving our understanding of neural coding, brain organisation, and how information can be decoded from brain activity.

In the 1980s, Apostolos Georgopoulos made significant contributions to motor decoding research by discovering motor tuning curves. This showed that neurons in the motor cortex can respond differently depending on the direction of intended movement.

This discovery demonstrated that brain signals contain information about movement intentions. By analysing these signals, researchers could begin developing systems capable of interpreting a person's intended actions.

An important question in BCI research is the difference between invasive and non-invasive approaches. The choice depends on the goal of the technology.

Non-invasive methods such as EEG can measure brain activity from outside the skull, while invasive methods place electrodes closer to neurons to obtain higher-resolution signals.

A useful analogy is a football stadium. Standing outside the stadium, you may hear the crowd and understand whether something exciting is happening, but you cannot know the exact details of the game.

However, placing a microphone closer to the players allows you to capture more detailed information. Similarly, invasive BCIs place recording devices closer to neurons to obtain more precise brain signals.

Ultimately, BCI technology focuses on understanding where signals should be collected and how that information can be used to improve communication, healthcare, and human-computer interaction.

Timeline

The development of Brain-Computer Interfaces has progressed from early discoveries of brain activity to modern implantable technologies capable of restoring communication and controlling digital devices.

1924

First Human EEG Recording

German psychiatrist Hans Berger successfully recorded the first human EEG signals. His invention of the electroencephalograph laid the scientific foundation for modern Brain-Computer Interface research.

1977

First Laboratory BCI Experiment

Researchers demonstrated one of the earliest Brain-Computer Interface systems, using EEG signals to move a computer cursor. This showed that brain activity could be translated into computer commands.

1988

Brain-Controlled Robot

Stevo Bozinovski and colleagues successfully controlled a robot using EEG signals. This milestone demonstrated that brain activity could be used to operate a physical device in real time.

2011

Wireless EEG Headsets

EMOTIV introduced one of the first commercially available wireless EEG headsets, making Brain-Computer Interface technology more accessible for research, education, and consumer applications.

2017

Brain-Controlled Formula One Car

Rodrigo Hübner Mendes became the first quadriplegic person to drive a Formula One car using only brain signals captured by an EEG headset, demonstrating the real-world potential of BCI technology.

2024

Neuralink Human Demonstration

Neuralink demonstrated an implanted Brain-Computer Interface that enabled a participant to control a computer cursor and play video games using only brain signals, highlighting recent advances in implantable BCI systems.
