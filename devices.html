1. Non-Invasive BCIs

Non-invasive BCIs measure brain activity using sensors placed on the scalp, allowing users to interact with computers without requiring surgery.

Did you know?

The human brain remains continuously active therefore approximately 86 billion neurons generate electrochemical impulses at all times, collectively producing measurable electrical activity detectable at the scalp.

Source: The Guardian Human brain neurons article

Examples of Non-invasive BCI Acquisition Methods

1.1 Electroencephalogram (EEG) signals

What is EEG?

It measures the brain's electrical activity. Small electrodes placed on the scalp record electrical potentials produced by electrical current flow in and around neurons . These recordings allow researchers and clinicians to observe brain activity without requiring surgery [1].

1.2 Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (FMRI) signals

fMRI is a kind of MRI, usually used to deliver structural pictures of human body. In the specialised application of MRI, it is used to produce tomographic images of variations in hemodynamic responses which represent the temporal changes in neural activity. In fMRI, a high Tesla magnetic field combined with radiofrequency (RF) pulse and magnetic gradient is used to image the patient's brain, based on the principle of NMR. It is neither radioactive nor invasive with few known side effects

2. Minimal-Invasive BCIs

A surgery is required to open the skull, however the electrodes sit on the surface of the brain rather than penetrating into it.

Example of Minimal-invasive method


2.1 Electrocorticography (ECoG)

ECoG is a partially invasive BCI recording technique that uses electrode arrays placed directly on the surface of the cerebral cortex to capture brain activity. It provides a balance between signal quality and safety by offering higher spatial and temporal resolution compared with non-invasive methods such as EEG, while avoiding deeper brain tissue penetration associated with fully invasive implants.[3]

3. Invasive BCIs

Invasive BCIs involve surgical implantation of electrodes directly into the brain tissue. These electrodes connect to neurons and can record brain signals with high precision.[5]

Example of Invasive method


3.1 Utah Array

Utah array implant system containing multiple electrode arrays designed to record neural activity from the brain. The implant uses microelectrodes positioned within brain tissue to capture high-resolution electrical signals.
