Why Cybersecurity Matters in Brain-Computer Interfaces

As Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) become more widely used, protecting them from cyber threats becomes increasingly important.

  • BCIs are used in healthcare, research, communication, and other advanced applications.
  • Unlike traditional computers, BCIs process neural data (brain signals), which may reveal a person's thoughts, emotions, or medical conditions.
  • Since neural data is highly sensitive, it must be protected from cyber threats to preserve user privacy and safety.
  • Many modern BCIs communicate through wireless networks and cloud services, creating new cybersecurity risks.
  • Attackers may attempt to:
    • Intercept brain signals during transmission.
    • Gain unauthorized access to BCI devices.
    • Manipulate communication between the brain and external systems.
    • Steal or misuse stored neural data.
  • Strong cybersecurity measures, including encryption, authentication, and secure software updates, are essential to protect users and their neural data.

"The brain is the final frontier of privacy."

Nita Farahany, Professor of Law and Neuroethics, Duke University

Common Cybersecurity Threats to Brain-Computer Interfaces

As BCIs exchange neural data with external devices, they may be exposed to several cybersecurity risks. The table below summarises some of the most common threats identified in current research.

Cybersecurity Threat Description Possible Impact
Neural Data Interception Attackers intercept brain signals while they are transmitted between the BCI and external devices. Loss of privacy and exposure of sensitive neural information.
Unauthorized Device Access Hackers gain access to a BCI through weak authentication or software vulnerabilities. Changes to device settings or unauthorized control.
Malware Attacks Malicious software infects the BCI system or connected computer. Reduced performance, incorrect outputs, or device malfunction.
Signal Manipulation Neural signals are altered or spoofed before reaching the intended system. Incorrect commands or unsafe device behaviour.
Cloud Data Breaches Stored neural data is accessed through compromised cloud services. Large-scale leakage of users' brain data.

Physical Health Risks of Brain-Computer Interfaces

"I knew there were a lot of risks going in, and I knew it might not work. I didn't anticipate any of that though. I had complete faith in Neuralink."

Noland Arbaugh, the first person to receive a Neuralink brain implant (2024)

While Brain-Computer Interfaces can improve communication, movement, and quality of life, some devices require surgery to implant electrodes.
As with any medical procedure, implantation carries physical risks that should be carefully considered.
These risks are generally greater for partially invasive and invasive BCIs compared to non-invasive BCIs.

Physical Risks of Brain-Computer Interfaces

While non-invasive BCIs have very few physical risks, partially invasive and fully invasive BCIs require surgery and may lead to medical complications.

Physical Risk Description Real Example / Further Reading
Infection Brain surgery may introduce bacteria, causing infections around the implant or surrounding brain tissue. Case Western Reserve University – Brain implant infections
Bleeding (Hemorrhage) Implantation can damage small blood vessels, resulting in bleeding inside the brain. Medical Xpress – Risks of brain implants
Scar Tissue Formation The body's immune system may form scar tissue around the electrodes, reducing signal quality over time. Brain-Computer Interface: Trends, Challenges and Threats
Device Failure Hardware components may wear out, malfunction, or require additional surgery for repair or replacement. Brain-Computer Interface: An Update for Clinicians
Brain Tissue Damage Implanting electrodes may injure nearby neurons or brain tissue, especially during surgery. Safety concerns in invasive BCIs
