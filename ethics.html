Where Gaming Meets Neuroscience

Brain-Computer Interfaces are changing the way people interact with video games by allowing actions to be controlled through brain activity instead of traditional controllers.

Researchers at the University of Calgary are exploring BCI-based games that help children with physical disabilities enjoy gaming using only their brain signals.[1]

Using EEG headsets, brain signals are detected, analysed, and converted into commands that allow users to interact with digital environments.

As this technology develops, BCI gaming could combine artificial intelligence and virtual reality to create more personalised experiences. Watch video below to learn more how are BCI being used in gaming field.

Education

How BCIs Are Transforming Education [2]

  • Monitor student attention by measuring brain activity during lessons.
  • Support personalized learning by helping teachers adapt lessons to individual needs.
  • Improve student engagement through real-time feedback on focus and concentration.
  • Track learning progress using students' cognitive and emotional data.
  • Create more interactive classrooms by allowing teachers to adjust lessons.
  • Support students with disabilities by enabling them to interact with computers using brain signals.

Where Robotics Meets Neuroscience

BCI technology can also support rehabilitation by allowing users to control machines and robotic devices using brain signals. Non-invasive BCI systems, such as those using Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (NIRS), can detect brain activity without requiring surgery.
This technology has been explored to help physically disabled individuals control robotic arms and assistive devices, improving independence and rehabilitation opportunities. [3]

Where Military Meets Neuroscience

How BCIs Could Transform Military Operations [4]

  • Human-Machine Interaction: BCIs could allow soldiers to communicate directly with machines and improve cooperation between humans and technology.
  • Drone and System Control: BCIs may help operators control unmanned systems and military equipment using brain signals.
  • Improved Decision-Making: BCIs could monitor cognitive workload and support faster decision-making in complex environments.
  • Enhanced Communication: Future BCI systems may provide new ways for soldiers to exchange information quickly.
  • Support for Injured Soldiers: BCI technology can assist with advanced prosthetics and rehabilitation for military personnel who have lost physical abilities.
